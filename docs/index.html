21cmFAST

Introduction

21cmFAST is a semi-numerical simulation framework that models the high-redshift 21-cm signal across various environments. Traditional N-body simulations and coupled radiative transfer calculations demand massive computational time which significantly limits the scope of the work. Rapid parameter space exploration is innately difficult and 21cmFAST avoids exact N-body gravity calculations. Bypassing these calculations involves using linear perturbation theory and the excursion set formalism instead which approximate density, velocity, and ionization fields. Fast Fourier Transforms (FFTs) provide the necessary computational speed and they filter spatial fields quickly across different physical scales. Therefore, generating large-scale volumes which happens much faster than conventional methods. High resolution remains possible for the final data. Thus, physical properties without severe numerical costs are observed.

Cubes

This simulation structures cosmological data inside discrete 3D spatial grids and it uses comoving coordinates for all spatial axes. If we scale these volumes accurately, which requires the evaluation of the line-of-sight comoving distance $D_c(z)$ to a redshift $z$, that is done by integrating the inverse of the Hubble parameter $H(z)$. In this context, $c$ represents the speed of light. $H_0$ is the present-day Hubble constant. $E(z)$ acts as the dimensionless Hubble parameter which governs the expansion rate for the whole system. The expansion accelerates at lower redshifts due to dark energy. However, these equations exhibit a strict dependence on several fundamental parameters: $\Omega_m$ represents the total matter density parameter, $\Omega_\Lambda$ which defines the dark energy density parameter, and we assume a flat cosmology where $\Omega_k = 0$. Determining the spatial dimensions of the simulation cubes requires these parameters. In conjunction to the existing parameters, we also need the reduced Hubble parameter $h = H_0 / (100 \text{ km s}^{-1} \text{ Mpc}^{-1})$ to finish this setup.

Brightness Temperature

We have the observable 21-cm signal quantified as the differential brightness temperature, $\delta T_b$. It represents the intensity contrast between the intergalactic neutral hydrogen and the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) radiation. The formula for the signal at a comoving coordinate $\mathbf{r}$ and redshift $z$ happens via the following equation:

$$\delta T_b(\mathbf{r}, z) \approx 27 x_{HI}(\mathbf{r}, z) [1 + \delta(\mathbf{r}, z)] \left( \frac{\Omega_b h^2}{0.023} \right) \sqrt{\frac{0.15}{\Omega_m h^2} \frac{1+z}{10}} \left( 1 - \frac{T_{CMB}(z)}{T_S(\mathbf{r}, z)} \right) [\text{mK}]$$

This formulation separates the signal into its constituents $x_{HI}(\mathbf{r}, z)$ is the local neutral hydrogen fraction, $\delta(\mathbf{r}, z)$ acts as the local baryon overdensity, the global baryon density parameter as $\Omega_b$, $T_S(\mathbf{r}, z)$ is the local spin temperature of the hydrogen gas. The Wouthuysen-Field effect governs this spin temperature. It couples $T_S$ to the local kinetic temperature via Ly-$\alpha$ photon scattering. User defined parameters in the simulation dictate the evolution of $T_S$ and $x_{HI}$. The absorption occurs when spin temperature is lower than CMB temperature. These variables determine mathematically if the signal shows emission or absorption.

Lightcones

Radio interferometers do not measure a static 3D volume of the universe but they observe continuously along a line of sight. It is noticed that greater comoving distances correlate directly to earlier cosmic epochs. Simulations of this observational reality accurately involves joining discrete co-eval volumes into a continuous structure. This structure is known as a lightcone. The mapping between the line-of-sight spatial coordinate, $r_\parallel$, and the observed redshift, $z$, depends on the differential comoving distance:

$$dr_\parallel = \frac{c}{H(z)} dz$$

Generating the lightcone uses static 3D data cubes at discrete redshift intervals. This lightcone acts as an array of slices from different time-steps. Extracting 2D transverse spatial slices from these cubes happens at equal comoving distance intervals. The simulation assembles them along the $r_\parallel$ axis. Therefore, this mechanism produces a volume where the intergalactic medium (IGM) evolves dynamically along the depth axis which mimics the light travel time effects directly.

Frequency Spectra

The hyperfine spin-flip transition of neutral hydrogen originates the 21-cm signal which happens at a rest-frame frequency of $\nu_{21} \approx 1420.40575$ MHz. Due to the cosmic expansion, these emitted photons lose energy before reaching our observation instruments. Observed frequency scales inversely with redshift. The direct relationship between the observed frequency, $\nu_{obs}$, and the cosmological redshift, $z$, is given by:

$$\nu_{obs} = \frac{\nu_{21}}{1 + z}$$

The lower observed frequencies correspond strictly to higher redshifts. Radio telescopes record the 21-cm signal as a continuous spectrum across multiple frequency bands. However, generating the macroscopic time evolution of the entire IGM requires the spatial volume average of the 3D brightness temperature grid and we do this here at each redshift step.

This reduction highlights the macroscopic progression of the Epoch of Reionization (EoR). As the global neutral hydrogen fraction drops toward zero, the global differential signal extinguishes.

Power Spectra

The quantification of the spatial variance uses transforming the 3D brightness temperature field $\delta T_b(\mathbf{r})$ into Fourier space. This produces $\tilde{\delta T_b}(\mathbf{k})$, where the term $\mathbf{k}$ is the comoving wavevector. The small values of $k$ correspond to large physical scales. The 1D spherically averaged power spectrum, $P(k)$, defines the variance as a function of the scalar wavenumber $k = |\mathbf{k}|$. This corresponds to the inverse of the physical structural scale. We compute it by the following ensemble average of the Fourier modes:

$$\langle \tilde{\delta T_b}(\mathbf{k}) \tilde{\delta T_b}^*(\mathbf{k}') \rangle = (2\pi)^3 \delta_D(\mathbf{k} - \mathbf{k}') P(k)$$

Here, the $\delta_D$ is the Dirac delta function. The standard visualizations plot the dimensionless power spectrum, $\Delta^2(k) = k^3 P(k) / (2\pi^2)$. In conjunction to this, the observed physical signal exhibits structural anisotropy due to Redshift-Space Distortions (RSD) because the peculiar velocities of infalling gas clouds alter their apparent spatial positions along the line-of-sight. We can counter this by the decomposition of the wavevector into perpendicular ($k_\perp$) and parallel ($k_\parallel$) components. Therefore, we calculate the 2D cylindrically averaged power spectrum, $P(k_\perp, k_\parallel)$, by averaging the Fourier modes strictly within cylindrical annuli.
