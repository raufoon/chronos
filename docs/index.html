21cmFAST

Introduction

21cmFAST is a highly efficient, semi-numerical simulation framework developed to model the high-redshift 21-cm signal generated during the Cosmic Dawn and the Epoch of Reionization (EoR) [1]. Traditional N-body and fully coupled radiative transfer simulations are computationally prohibitive, making rapid parameter space exploration difficult. 21cmFAST circumvents the need for exact N-body gravity calculations and ray-tracing by utilizing linear perturbation theory and the excursion set formalism to approximate density, velocity, and ionization fields [2]. The computational speed of the simulation is primarily derived from its extensive use of Fast Fourier Transforms (FFTs), which are employed to rapidly filter spatial fields across various physical scales. This approach allows researchers to generate large-scale statistical volumes of the universe orders of magnitude faster than conventional simulations, while maintaining a rigorous adherence to the underlying physics.

Cubes

The cosmological data is structured within discrete 3D spatial grids constructed in comoving coordinates. To accurately scale these volumes, the line-of-sight comoving distance $r(z)$ to a given redshift $z$ is evaluated by integrating the inverse of the Hubble parameter $H(z)$ over the expansion history of the universe [4]:

$$r(z) = \frac{c}{H_0} \int_{0}^{z} \frac{dz'}{E(z')}$$

where $c$ is the speed of light, $H_0$ is the present-day Hubble constant, and $E(z)$ is the dimensionless Hubble parameter governing the expansion rate:

$$E(z) = \sqrt{\Omega_m(1+z)^3 + \Omega_k(1+z)^2 + \Omega_\Lambda}$$

These equations depend on several fundamental input parameters: $\Omega_m$ represents the total matter density parameter (incorporating both cold dark matter and baryons), $\Omega_\Lambda$ defines the dark energy density parameter, and $\Omega_k = 1 - \Omega_m - \Omega_\Lambda$ accounts for the spatial curvature of the universe. In standard 21cmFAST configurations, a flat cosmology ($\Omega_k = 0$) is typically assumed. The spatial dimensions of the simulation cubes are determined by these parameters alongside the reduced Hubble parameter $h = H_0 / (100 \text{ km s}^{-1} \text{ Mpc}^{-1})$.

Brightness Temperature

The observable 21-cm signal is quantified as the differential brightness temperature, $\delta T_b$, representing the intensity contrast between the intergalactic neutral hydrogen and the background Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) radiation [5]. The spatially-dependent state of the signal at a comoving coordinate $\mathbf{r}$ and redshift $z$ is given by:

$$\delta T_b(\mathbf{r}, z) \approx 27 x_{HI}(\mathbf{r}, z) [1 + \delta(\mathbf{r}, z)] \left( \frac{\Omega_b h^2}{0.023} \right) \sqrt{\frac{0.15}{\Omega_m h^2} \frac{1+z}{10}} \left( 1 - \frac{T_{CMB}(z)}{T_S(\mathbf{r}, z)} \right) [\text{mK}]$$

This formulation separates the signal into its core astrophysical drivers. The term $x_{HI}(\mathbf{r}, z)$ represents the local neutral hydrogen fraction. The parameter $\delta(\mathbf{r}, z)$ is the local baryon overdensity, with $\Omega_b$ acting as the global baryon density parameter. The local spin temperature of the hydrogen gas is denoted by $T_S(\mathbf{r}, z)$. The spin temperature is governed by the Wouthuysen-Field effect [10], which couples $T_S$ to the local kinetic temperature via Ly-$\alpha$ photon scattering. In the simulation, user-defined parameters such as X-ray heating efficiency and the ionizing photon escape fraction directly dictate the evolution of $T_S$ and $x_{HI}$, which mathematically govern whether the localized signal exhibits emission, absorption, or is entirely extinguished.

Lightcones

Radio interferometers do not measure instantaneous, static 3D volumes of the universe; they observe continuously along a line of sight where greater comoving distances correlate directly with earlier cosmic epochs. To accurately simulate this observational reality, discrete co-eval volumes must be stitched into a continuous "lightcone". The mapping between the line-of-sight spatial coordinate, $r_\parallel$, and the observed redshift, $z$, is determined by the differential comoving distance:

$$dr_\parallel = \frac{c}{H(z)} dz$$

To generate the lightcone, the simulation calculates several static 3D data cubes at discrete redshift intervals. It then interpolates and extracts 2D transverse spatial slices from these consecutive cubes, assembling them along the $r_\parallel$ axis. This computational stitching produces a volume where the physical state of the intergalactic medium dynamically evolves along the depth axis, accurately mimicking the light travel time effects measured by an observer.

Frequency Spectra

The 21-cm signal originates from the hyperfine spin-flip transition of neutral hydrogen, which occurs at a rest-frame frequency of $\nu_{21} \approx 1420.40575$ MHz. Due to cosmic expansion, these emitted photons are redshifted before reaching observation instruments. The direct relationship between the observed frequency, $\nu_{obs}$, and the cosmological redshift, $z$, is given by:

$$\nu_{obs} = \frac{\nu_{21}}{1 + z}$$

Because lower observed frequencies correspond strictly to higher redshifts, radio telescopes record the cosmological 21-cm signal as a continuous spectrum across multiple frequency bands. By calculating the spatial volume average of the 3D brightness temperature grid at each discrete redshift step, the simulation generates the macroscopic temporal evolution of the entire intergalactic medium.

Power Spectra

To quantify the spatial variance and underlying patchiness of the 21-cm signal, the 3D brightness temperature field $\delta T_b(\mathbf{r})$ is transformed into Fourier space, producing $\tilde{\delta T_b}(\mathbf{k})$, where $\mathbf{k}$ represents the comoving wavevector. The 1D spherically averaged power spectrum, $P(k)$, defines the variance as a function of the scalar wavenumber $k = |\mathbf{k}|$ (in units of Mpc$^{-1}$), which corresponds to the inverse of the physical structural scale. It is computed via the ensemble average of the Fourier modes:

$$\langle \tilde{\delta T_b}(\mathbf{k}) \tilde{\delta T_b}^*(\mathbf{k}') \rangle = (2\pi)^3 \delta_D(\mathbf{k} - \mathbf{k}') P(k)$$

where $\delta_D$ is the Dirac delta function. Standard visualizations plot the dimensionless power spectrum, $\Delta^2(k) = k^3 P(k) / (2\pi^2)$, which measures the contribution to the total spatial variance per logarithmic interval of $k$.

However, the observed physical signal is structurally anisotropic due to Redshift-Space Distortions (RSD) [6], where the peculiar velocities of infalling gas clouds alter their apparent line-of-sight spatial positions. To rigorously separate this kinematic effect from transverse spatial clustering [7], the wavevector is decomposed into perpendicular ($k_\perp$) and parallel ($k_\parallel$) components, such that $k^2 = k_\perp^2 + k_\parallel^2$. The 2D cylindrically averaged power spectrum, $P(k_\perp, k_\parallel)$, is then calculated by averaging the Fourier modes strictly within cylindrical annuli, enabling cosmologists to isolate structures observed across the plane of the sky from those distributed along the frequency depth axis.
